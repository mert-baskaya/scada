One pipeline, four faults, zero duplicates

A simulated electrical grid streams telemetry through Flink CEP into Postgres. We killed its TaskManager, cut its network, stopped its broker, restarted its coordinator — then pushed it to a million events per second. Every number below is measured, not theoretical.

01

The pipeline

Five stages, three Kafka topics. Ten grid components (feeders, breakers, transformers across two substations) emit one reading per second each.

Read the edges: throughput drops five-hundred-fold across the job — 10 raw events/s in, roughly 10 aggregate rows per 30 s plus occasional alerts out. The simulator's acks=0 producer means anything upstream of Kafka is fire-and-forget.

02

Inside the Flink job

One shared source fans out into five branches. Four are CEP pattern matchers; each is badged with its key and match window. Only one pattern correlates across devices.

The dashed branch is the interesting one: B keys by substationId, not componentId — a feeder's voltage sag and a different device's breaker trip are matched inside one pattern. Hover any node for its exact condition.

03

Where the latency lives

Worst-case path from a telemetry event to a visible aggregate row. Exactly-once is the smallest slice — the window itself dominates.

Alerts skip the 30 s slice — CEP emits as soon as a pattern completes — but still pay the watermark and checkpoint-commit slices. That commit slice is the price of the zero-duplicates result in §06.

04

Four faults, measured

Each lane starts at the moment of injection. Ticks are the 10 s checkpoint cadence — the maximum amount of reprocessing any recovery ever needs.

fault / hard down undetected — job still reports RUNNING recovering healthy

Hover any segment for the evidence behind it. The contrast to notice: a killed process is detected in ~20 s via TCP connection loss, but a silent one costs the full 50 s heartbeat timeout. And the Kafka lane never leaves RUNNING — resilience is layered, and the connector's retry layer sits below the restart strategy.

Offsets, state, and output move atomically — all four links restore to the same checkpoint, which is why replayed records never become duplicate rows.

07

Then we asked for a million events per second

A Java load generator replaced the 10 ev/s simulator: 10,000 components across 500 substations, realistic anomaly episodes, produce rate held within ±0.05% of target. The job now runs in three modes (PIPELINE_MODE) — same detection semantics, very different physics.

why cep dies
Buffer ∝ rate × disorder
CEP sorts by event time, so it holds every event for the 5 s out-of-orderness bound — ×4 patterns. At 1M ev/s that's tens of millions of live heap objects.
the fix
Pre-filter the stream
The patterns only match rare events: sags, OPEN breakers, oil > 90 °C, status transitions. Filter first and CEP buffers hundreds of events/s, not a million.
the exception
consecutive() semantics
SUSTAINED_OVERCURRENT's times(5).consecutive() is defined over every reading — filtering changes its meaning, so it became a keyed process function.
the receipt
3–4 ms/s busy
The operators that crashed the job at 200k are ~0.3% busy at 1M. All four alert types verified flowing; aggregates complete: 3002 readings/window × 10k components = 1M/s exactly.

The real-world lesson: don't make CEP fast, make it cheap. CEP cost is proportional to what it buffers, not what the pipeline ingests — pre-filtering is a 10–50× lever, watermark tightness the second.
